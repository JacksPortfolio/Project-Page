Analyzing Traffic Signals and Motor Vehicle Collisions in Toronto

Introduction

In my research, I looked at the Toronto traffic light and car accident databases. Information about driving methods, pedestrian signals, bicycle signs and other traffic sign functions can be found in the Liikenneopasteet database. In addition to accident conditions and severity, the motor vehicle accident database also contains information on accident types, severity of injuries and control methods at intersections.

Insights and Analysis

Collision Severity Distribution:

Most collisions resulted in no injuries (46.92%) or minor injuries (34.00%). Fatal collisions constituted a small percentage (5.15%).

Impact of Control Modes on Collision Severity:

The SA2 control mode had the highest total collision count (1,190 incidents), including the highest major injury collisions (384).

The PA-MPS mode had a significant proportion of fatal collisions (20 out of 114 incidents), indicating potential issues with pedestrian management.

Impact Type Analysis:

Collisions while going ahead were the most frequent, with 6,542 incidents, including 278 fatalities.

Collisions with no specified impact type were also high (7,953 incidents), highlighting a need for better data recording practices.

Approaches at Intersections:

Most intersections had three and five approaches, with four being the most common (1,866,687), suggesting that four-way intersections are the norm in Toronto.

Vehicle Involvement:

Automobiles had the highest collision rate, accounting for 78.52% of all incidents.

Potential Real-World Applications

Urban Planning and Traffic Management:

Planners can prioritize upgrades to high-collision intersections.

Safety Campaigns:

Public awareness campaigns can target behaviours leading to high collision rates.

Infrastructure Improvements:

Expanding the installation of audible and bicycle signals can enhance safety for pedestrians and cyclists.

Conclusion

This project highlights how data analysis and visualization can uncover insights that drive meaningful urban safety improvements. Additionally, the findings can inform policy decisions to reduce collision rates and improve overall traffic safety in Toronto.
